    Giant Component¶

    1. What this topic is about¶

    In \(G(n, p)\) with \(p = c/n\), when \(c > 1\) a unique component of size \(\Theta(n)\) emerges — the giant. Its asymptotic size fraction \(\theta\) is the survival probability of a Poisson(\(c\)) branching process. The standard proof tracks BFS exploration as a stochastic process and shows it concentrates around a deterministic fluid limit \(f(x) = 1 - e^{-cx} - x\).

    2. Core objects / definitions¶

    • \(G(n, p)\): each pair of vertices is an edge independently with probability \(p\).
    • Sparse regime: \(p = c/n\) for fixed \(c > 0\).
    • BFS exploration of \(C(x)\): maintain Removed / Active / Unexplored. At step \(t+1\), pop one active vertex, reveal its neighbors among unexplored.
    • Active queue size: \(\tilde Y_t\) with \(\tilde Y_0 = 1\), \(\tilde Y_{t+1} = \tilde Y_t + X_{t+1} - 1\).
    • Step revelation: \(X_{t+1} \mid \mathcal{F}_t \sim \text{Bin}(N_t, c/n)\), where \(N_t\) = number of unexplored.
    • Component size: \(|C(x)| = \min\{t : \tilde Y_t = 0\} = \min\{t : \tilde Y_t \leq 0\}\).
    • Giant fraction \(\theta\): non-trivial solution of \(\theta + e^{-c\theta} = 1\), equivalently the unique \(\theta \in (0,1)\) with \(f(\theta) = 0\) where \(f(x) = 1 - e^{-cx} - x\).

    3. Standard proof moves¶

    1. Set up BFS exploration, write the \(\tilde Y_t\) recurrence.
    2. Find the binomial: show \(N_t \sim \text{Bin}(n-1, (1-c/n)^t)\) via the "each unexplored vertex independently survives each round" coupling. Then \(\Delta_t := n-1-N_t \sim \text{Bin}(n-1, 1 - (1-c/n)^t)\) and \(\tilde Y_t = \Delta_t + 1 - t\).
    3. Apply multiplicative Chernov to \(\Delta_t\) (NOT additive — need relative bound, since \(\mathbb{E}[\Delta_t]\) varies in \(t\)).
    4. Sandwich with \(f_{\pm\epsilon}\): \(f_{-\epsilon}(x) = (1-\epsilon)(1-e^{-cx}) - x \leq \tilde Y_t/n \leq f_{+\epsilon}(x) + O(1/n) = (1+\epsilon)(1-e^{-cx}) - x + O(1/n)\) w.h.p.
    5. Analyze zeros of \(f_{\pm\epsilon}\): each is strictly convex, has exactly one nontrivial zero \(x_\pm(\epsilon)\), and \(x_\pm(\epsilon) \to \theta\) as \(\epsilon \to 0\).
    6. Convert to bounds on \(|C(x)|\): lower from sandwich ⇒ \(\tilde Y_t > 0\) until \(t \approx (\theta-\delta)n\); upper from sandwich ⇒ \(\tilde Y_t < 0\) by \(t \approx (\theta+\delta)n\).
    7. Union bound over \(t \in [K\log n, n]\): the geometric tail of \(e^{-\epsilon^2 t / 8}\) gives \(O(e^{-\epsilon^2 K \log n / 8}) \to 0\), hence "w.h.p. for all \(t\) in range".

    4. Important lemmas / facts¶

    Lemma: BFS active queue is binomially distributed¶

    Statement. \(N_t \sim \text{Bin}(n-1, (1-c/n)^t)\), so \(\Delta_t = X_1 + \dots + X_t \sim \text{Bin}(n-1, 1 - (1-c/n)^t)\), and $\(\tilde Y_t = \Delta_t + 1 - t.\)$

    Why it matters. The whole concentration argument runs through this. Without a binomial form, you can't apply Chernov.

    Intuition. Each unexplored vertex independently survives each round with prob \(1-c/n\).

    Proof idea. By induction on \(t\). Conditional on \(N_{t-1}\), \(X_t \sim \text{Bin}(N_{t-1}, c/n)\) so \(N_t = N_{t-1} - X_t \sim \text{Bin}(N_{t-1}, 1-c/n)\). Thinning of binomial = binomial with multiplied parameter.

    Recognition signal. Anytime BFS or local exploration is happening on \(G(n,p)\), immediately convert to "each unexplored vertex independently survives".

    Common pitfall. Forgetting that \(N_t\) is binomial (not just "approximately Poisson") — you need the exact binomial form to apply multiplicative Chernov.

    Lemma: Fluid-limit sandwich¶

    Statement. For \(0 < \epsilon < 1\) and \(n\) large, with prob \(\geq 1 - O(e^{-\epsilon^2 t / 8})\), $\(f_{-\epsilon}(t/n) \leq \tilde Y_t / n \leq f_{+\epsilon}(t/n) + O(1/n).\)$

    Why it matters. Converts "stochastic process near its mean" into "process near a deterministic fluid limit." This is what lets you analyze zeros of \(f\) instead of zeros of the random process.

    Proof idea. Multiplicative Chernov on \(\Delta_t\) gives \(|\Delta_t - \mathbb{E}[\Delta_t]| \leq \epsilon \mathbb{E}[\Delta_t]\), then \(\mathbb{E}[\Delta_t]/n \to 1 - e^{-cx}\) by \(1 - (1 - c/n)^t = 1 - e^{-ct/n + O(t/n^2)}\).

    Recognition signal. Any "the random process tracks a deterministic curve w.h.p." statement.

    Lemma: \(\theta\) is the BP survival probability¶

    Statement. The non-trivial zero \(\theta\) of \(f(x) = 1 - e^{-cx} - x\) satisfies \(\theta + e^{-c\theta} = 1\), which is the same equation as the survival probability of a Poisson(\(c\)) branching process: \(1-\theta = e^{-c\theta}\) (since \(1-\theta\) is the extinction probability and equals the PGF of \(\text{Poi}(c)\) at \(1-\theta\)).

    Why it matters. Connects giant component to branching process — the foundational "BFS ≈ BP early on" picture.

    Intuition. Early in BFS exploration, the unexplored pool is essentially "infinite" (depleted by \(O(1/n)\)), so each new vertex has \(\approx \text{Bin}(n, c/n) \approx \text{Poi}(c)\) children. So local BFS = \(\text{Poi}(c)\) branching tree. The component survives ↔ BP survives ↔ probability \(\theta\).

    Lemma: Geometry of \(f_{\pm\epsilon}\)¶

    Statement. If \(\epsilon < 1 - 1/c\), then \(f_{-\epsilon}(x) = (1-\epsilon)(1-e^{-cx}) - x\) has exactly two zeros in \([0,1]\): \(x = 0\) and \(x_-(\epsilon) \in (0, 1)\). Moreover \(x_-(\epsilon) \leq \theta\) and \(x_-(\epsilon) \to \theta\) as \(\epsilon \to 0\). Symmetrically for \(f_{+\epsilon}\).

    Why it matters. Tells you exactly where the sandwich pinches the random process.

    Proof idea. \(f_{-\epsilon}'(0) = (1-\epsilon)c - 1 > 0\) when \(\epsilon < 1 - 1/c\), \(f_{-\epsilon}(1) < 0\), \(f_{-\epsilon}\) is strictly convex ⇒ exactly one nontrivial zero. Convergence \(x_-(\epsilon) \to \theta\) uses \(f\) is strictly convex with \(f(\theta)=0\), so \(f(\theta - \delta) > 0\), and \(f_{-\epsilon}(\theta - \delta) \to f(\theta - \delta) > 0\) by continuity in \(\epsilon\).

    5. Common mistakes¶

    • Using additive Chernov when multiplicative is required.
    • Forgetting \(|C(x)| \geq K\log n\) conditioning — small components are dominated by branching-process noise, not the fluid limit.
    • Treating the trivial zero \(x=0\) as the answer for component size — always go for the non-trivial zero \(\theta\).
    • Forgetting that \(\theta > 0\) requires \(c > 1\) (else \(f'(0) = c - 1 \leq 0\) and the only root is \(x=0\)).
    • Confusing "fraction of unexplored remaining" \((1-c/n)^t\) with "Poisson(\(ct/n\)) probability of being missed". They agree to leading order but the binomial is the rigorous form.

    6. Quick memory hooks¶

    • "BFS active queue does a Poisson(\(c\)) branching process — until the unexplored pool runs out." The \(-x\) term is the depletion correction.
    • \(f(x) = 1 - e^{-cx} - x\): "fraction explored minus fraction popped."
    • "\(\theta\) is where the queue would deterministically die." Past \(\theta n\), the depletion overwhelms the offspring rate.
    • Sandwich plot: draw \(f\) as a strictly convex hill that starts at 0, rises to a peak, then dips back below 0 at \(\theta\). \(f_{\pm\epsilon}\) are slightly squashed/stretched copies that pinch \(\theta\) from both sides as \(\epsilon \to 0\).

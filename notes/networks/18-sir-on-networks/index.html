    SIR on Networks¶

    Companion to SIR and Epidemics.md (homogeneous mixing); this note covers SIR on \(G(n,p)\) and the configuration model, plus the SIR ODEs.

    Setup: Infection digraph¶

    Each vertex draws recovery time \(T_i\sim\mu\) i.i.d. Conditional on \(\{T_i\}\), for each edge \(ij\) independently draw it as "open" with prob \(p_{T_i} = 1-e^{-\beta T_i}\). \(C_+(v)=R(\infty)=\) vertices ever-infected starting from \(v\) = those reachable in the infection digraph.

    Theorem 1 (Bow-tie / two-stage BP for \(G(n,p)\) SIR, \(p=c/n\))¶

    For homogeneous mixing on \(K_{n+1}\) or \(G(n,p)\): - If \(R_0 = c\mathbb E[p_T] < 1\): \(R_\infty\) is bounded in probability. - If \(R_0 > 1\): \(\Pr(R_\infty\geq n^{1/3}) \to P>0\), and conditioned on \(R_\infty\geq n^{1/3}\), \(R_\infty/n\to\theta>0\) where $\(\theta + e^{-\theta R_0} = 1,\quad\text{equivalently}\quad 1-P = \mathbb E_T[e^{-cP\,p_T}].\)$

    Proof sketch (BFS + concentration). Track \(N_t\) = #susceptibles after \(t\) infections, \(\Delta_t\) = #ever-infected. Each newly infected node draws \(X_t\sim\text{Bin}(N_{t-1}, \tfrac{c}{n}p_{T_t})\) new infections. By independence the BP approximation \(X_t^+\sim\text{Bin}(n, \tfrac{c}{n}p_T)\) couples to \(X_t\) with error \(O(t/n)\), valid for \(t\leq n^{1/3}\). Subcriticality (rate function bound, see the SIR and Epidemics note) gives \(\Pr(R_\infty\geq k)\leq e^{-(1-c)^2 k/4}\). Above criticality, BFS leaves the BP regime and a deterministic LLN takes over: \(\Delta_t/n\to f(t/n)\) on the rescaled time, fixed point \(\theta+e^{-c\theta}=1\) (general \(\mu\): replace \(c\) by \(R_0\)).

    Rigorous proof of the supercritical fluid limit¶

    Lemma 7.2. There exists \(\alpha>0\) such that for \(n\) large, \(R_0>1\), and \(0<\epsilon\leq 1/2\), with prob \(\geq 1 - O(e^{-\alpha\epsilon^2 t})\): $\(f_{-\epsilon}(t/n) \leq \tilde Y_t/n \leq f_{+\epsilon}(t/n) + O(1/n),\quad f_{\pm\epsilon}(x) = (1\pm\epsilon)\big(1-e^{-(1\pm\epsilon)R_0 x}\big) - x.\)$

    Setup. \(\tilde Y_0=1\), \(\tilde Y_{t+1} = \tilde Y_t + X_{t+1} - 1\), with \(X_t\mid \mathcal{F}_{t-1}, T_t \sim \text{Bin}(N_{t-1}, p_t)\) where \(p_t = (c/n) p_{T_t}\) and \(p_{T_t} = 1 - e^{-\beta T_t}\). Then \(|C_+(x)| = \min\{t : \tilde Y_t \leq 0\}\).

    Why this is harder than the plain giant-component argument. Two layers of randomness: the durations \(T_i\) (which determine \(p_i\)), and the binomial neighbor draws given \(T_i\). We apply multiplicative Chernov twice — once on \(\sum p_i\) over the \(T_i\)'s, once on \(\Delta_t\) given the \(T_i\)'s.

    Proof.

    (i) Conditional binomial structure. By the same survival-coupling as in P2, conditional on \(T_1,\ldots,T_t\): $\(\Delta_t = X_1+\cdots+X_t \sim \text{Bin}\Big(n-1,\;1-\prod_{i=1}^t(1-p_i)\Big),\qquad \tilde Y_t = \Delta_t + 1 - t.\)$

    (ii) Sandwich \(\prod(1-p_i)\) and concentrate \(\sum p_i\). For \(n\geq 2c\) we have \(p_i\leq c/n\leq 1/2\), so Taylor of \(\log(1-p_i)\) gives $\(-p_i(1+c/n) \leq \log(1-p_i) \leq -p_i,\)$ and exponentiating: \(e^{-(1+c/n)\sum p_i} \leq \prod_{i=1}^t(1-p_i) \leq e^{-\sum p_i}\).

    Now \(\sum p_i = (c/n)\sum_i Y_i\) where \(Y_i = 1 - e^{-\beta T_i}\in[0,1]\) are i.i.d. with mean \(\bar p\). The multiplicative Chernov bound on \(\sum Y_i\) gives, with prob \(\geq 1 - 2e^{-\epsilon^2 \bar p t/4}\): $\((1-\epsilon)R_0\,\tfrac{t}{n} \leq \sum_{i=1}^t p_i \leq (1+\epsilon)R_0\,\tfrac{t}{n}\quad(R_0 := c\bar p).\)$ On this event: $\(e^{-(1+c/n)(1+\epsilon)R_0\,t/n} \leq \prod_{i=1}^t(1-p_i) \leq e^{-(1-\epsilon)R_0\,t/n}.\tag{7.6}\)$

    (iii) Concentrate \(\Delta_t\) given the \(T_i\)'s. On (7.6), use \(1-e^{-x}\geq x(1-e^{-1})\) for \(x\in[0,1]\): $\(\mathbb E[\Delta_t\mid T_1,\ldots,T_t] \geq (n-1)\big(1 - e^{-(1-\epsilon)R_0\,t/n}\big) \geq \tfrac{1-\epsilon}{2}t \geq t/4\quad(\epsilon\leq 1/2,\;n \text{ large}).\)$ Multiplicative Chernov (P1) applied to \(\Delta_t \mid T_1,\ldots,T_t\) then gives, with prob \(\geq 1 - 2e^{-\epsilon^2 t/16}\): $\((1-\epsilon)\,\mathbb E[\Delta_t\mid T_1,\ldots,T_t] \leq \Delta_t \leq (1+\epsilon)\,\mathbb E[\Delta_t\mid T_1,\ldots,T_t].\tag{7.7}\)$ Both events (7.6) and (7.7) hold with prob \(\geq 1 - O(e^{-\alpha\epsilon^2 t})\), \(\alpha = \min(\bar p/4, 1/16)\).

    (iv) Lower envelope. Combining (7.6) and (7.7): $\(\Delta_t \geq (1-\epsilon)(n-1)\big(1 - e^{-(1-\epsilon)R_0\,t/n}\big),\)$ so $\(\tilde Y_t \geq 1 - t + (1-\epsilon)(n-1)\big(1 - e^{-(1-\epsilon)R_0\,t/n}\big) \geq n\,f_{-\epsilon}(t/n).\)$

    (v) Upper envelope. On the upper side of (7.6): $\(\mathbb E[\Delta_t\mid T_1,\ldots,T_t] \leq (n-1)\big(1 - e^{-(1+\epsilon)(1+c/n)R_0\,t/n}\big) \leq n\big(1-e^{-(1+\epsilon)R_0\,t/n}\big) + O(t/n),\)$ where the second step uses \(e^{-(1+c/n)R_0 t/n} = e^{-R_0 t/n}\cdot e^{-O(t/n^2)}\) and \(t \leq n\). Combined with (7.7): $\(\tilde Y_t \leq 1 - t + (1+\epsilon)\mathbb E[\Delta_t\mid\cdot] \leq n(1+\epsilon)\big(1-e^{-(1+\epsilon)R_0 t/n}\big) - t + O(1) = n\big(f_{+\epsilon}(t/n) + O(1/n)\big).\)$

    This establishes Lemma 7.2. \(\square\)

    Corollary 7.3 (final outbreak size). If \(R_0>1\) and \(k_n\to\infty\) with \(k_n/n\to 0\), then conditioned on \(R_\infty\geq k_n\), \(R_\infty/n\to\theta\) in probability, where \(\theta+e^{-R_0\theta}=1\). Proof: repeat the giant-component argument (iv)–(viii) with \(c\) replaced by \(R_0\) — geometry of \(f_{\pm\epsilon}\) is structurally identical.

    Method extraction (two-layer Chernov). - Recognition. A process where each step has both a "type/weight" attached (here \(T_i\)) and an inner random outcome (here \(\text{Bin}(N_{t-1},p_i)\)). - First move. Condition on the types, get binomial inner structure. Then concentrate the sum-of-types separately as \([0,1]\)-valued sum via P1. - Pitfall. If you don't peel the layers, you'll end up with \(\sum(1-e^{-\beta T_i})\) inside an exponential and lose the Chernov hook. Also: \(p_i \leq c/n\) is required for the Taylor sandwich on \(\log(1-p_i)\) to drop higher-order terms. - Threshold takeaway. \(R_0 = c\bar p\) is the natural epidemic threshold — it's the offspring mean of the BFS-equivalent branching process averaged over recovery times.

    Lemma (Forward/backward BP perspective)¶

    For the bow-tie picture: let \(\eta = \Pr(\text{forward BP dies})\), \(\eta^* = \Pr(\text{backward BP dies})\). Then $\(1-P = G_+(1-P) = \mathbb E_T[e^{-cp_T P}],\qquad 1-\theta = G_-(1-\theta) = e^{-R_0\theta}.\)$ Here \(G_+, G_-\) are the offspring PGFs of forward/backward BPs, with \(G_-\) using degree-biased distribution.

    (Same fixed-point shape as homogeneous mixing; the network just changes the offspring law.)

    SIR ODE limit (homogeneous mixing, \(G(n,c/n)\))¶

    Let \(S(t)\) = #susceptibles, \(X(t)\) = #unexplored edges out of infectives, \(I(t)\) = #infected.

    Stochastic dynamics (Markov rates): - At rate \(\beta X\): explore one edge. With prob \(S/n\), the other endpoint is susceptible → it becomes infected, gains \(\text{Poi}(c)\) new edges. Either way \(X\to X-1\). - At rate \(\gamma\) each, an infective recovers (\(d_v\to 0\)).

    Theorem 2 (LLN for SIR ODE)¶

    As \(n\to\infty\), \((S/n, X/n, I/n)\to(s,x,i)\) deterministic, with $\(\dot s = -\beta x s,\qquad \dot x = -(\beta+\gamma)x - c\dot s,\qquad \dot i = -\gamma i - \dot s.\)$

    (Proof = differential-equation method à la Darling-Norris; quoted.)

    Lemma (Final size formula)¶

    \(s(\infty) = s(0)\,e^{-R_0(s(0)-s(\infty))}\) where \(R_0 = c\beta/(\beta+\gamma) = c\bar p\) (\(\bar p = \mathbb E[p_T]\)).

    Proof. From \(\dot x = -(\beta+\gamma)x - c\dot s\) integrate \(0\to\infty\) with \(x(0)=x(\infty)=0\): $\(0 = -(\beta+\gamma)\int_0^\infty x\,dt - c(s(\infty)-s(0))\;\Rightarrow\; \beta\int x = \tfrac{c\beta}{\beta+\gamma}(s(0)-s(\infty)).\)$ And \(\dot s/s = -\beta x\) gives \(s(\infty) = s(0)e^{-\beta\int x}\). Combine.

    SIR on Configuration Model CM(\(d\))¶

    Track: - \(X(t)\) = #active half-edges (incident to infectives, unmatched) - \(S_d(t)\) = #susceptible vertices of degree \(d\) - \(U(t)\) = #unmatched half-edges total

    Rate-of-change identities (when a \(\beta\)-clock rings, pair an active half-edge with a uniform unmatched one): $\(\dot U = -2\beta X,\qquad \dot S_d = -\beta d\,X/U,\qquad \dot X = -(\beta+\gamma)X - \beta X^2/U + \beta\sum_d d(d-1)S_d/U.\)$

    Lemma (Closed-form via \(\theta\)-reduction)¶

    Define \(\theta(t) = e^{-\beta\int_0^t X/U}\). Then $\(U = U(0)\theta^2 = n\bar d\,\theta^2,\qquad S_d = S_d(0)\theta^d = p_d n\,\theta^d.\)$

    Proof. \(\dot U/U = -2\beta X/U\) ⇒ integrate. \(\dot S_d/S_d = -\beta d\,X/U\) ⇒ integrate.

    Theorem 3 (Single ODE for \(\theta\))¶

    $\(\frac{1}{\beta+\gamma}\dot\theta = -F(\theta),\qquad F(\theta) = \bar p\big(1 - G^*(\theta)\big) - (1-\theta),\)$ where \(G^*(\theta) = \sum_k \tfrac{(k+1)p_{k+1}}{\bar d}\theta^k\) is the PGF of the size-biased excess degree, and \(\bar p = \beta/(\beta+\gamma)\).

    Proof sketch. Use \(\beta\sum_d d(d-1)S_d/U = \beta n\sum d(d-1)p_d\theta^{d-2}/(n\bar d) = \beta\,\partial_\theta G^*(\theta)\), plug into \(\dot X/X\), then with \(\psi:=\theta X/U\) derive a linear ODE in \(\psi\) with solution \(\psi=\tfrac{\beta+\gamma}{\beta}(\theta-1)-(G^*(\theta)-1)\). Combine with \(\dot\theta = -\beta\psi\).

    Lemma (Final outbreak size)¶

    Let \(\theta_\infty\) = smallest root of \(F(\theta)=0\) in \((0,1)\). Then $\(R_\infty/n \;\to\; 1 - G(\theta_\infty),\quad G(\theta)=\sum_k p_k\theta^k.\)$

    Why this matches forward/backward BP. \(1-\theta_\infty = \bar p(1-G^*(\theta_\infty))\) is exactly the backward-BP fixed point (\(\eta^* = G^*(1-\bar p(1-\eta^*))\) in disguise), and \(1-G(\theta_\infty)\) is the forward survival \(1-G(\eta^*\)-style pullback\()\).

    Force of infection / "infection runs out of high-degree first"¶

    $\(\frac{1}{S_k}\dot S_k = -\frac{k}{\theta}(\beta+\gamma)F(\theta).\)$ Force is proportional to \(k\) ⇒ high-degree susceptibles deplete fastest.

    Pitfalls¶

    • For CM, the excess degree \(G^*\) enters offspring (forward BP), not the raw \(G\).
    • \(R_0 = c\mathbb E[p_T] = c\bar p\); missing the \(\bar p\) factor (averaging over recovery time) is the most common slip.
    • \(\theta\) is not an extinction probability — it is the survival of an outgoing half-edge.
